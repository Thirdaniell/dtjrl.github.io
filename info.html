Setup Grades — What Each Grade Means

Every trade is graded based on how many ICT and Dodgy iFVG confluences align at the time of entry. Grading is strict — the goal is to only be taking A+ and above setups consistently.

A++
Elite Setup
The highest possible grade. Everything aligned perfectly — HTF bias, liquidity sweep, PDA entry, MSS/CHoCH, clear target, kill zone timing, and volume confirmation.
  • HTF bias confirmed top-down
  • Liquidity sweep (BSL/SSL) present
  • IFVG or FVG entry
  • MSS or CHoCH confirmation
  • Clear liquidity target
  • Kill zone timing
  • SMT divergence or volume
A+
Strong Setup
Nearly perfect. All major confluences present but missing one minor element (e.g. no kill zone, or no volume confirmation). These are high-confidence trades.
  • HTF bias confirmed
  • Liquidity sweep preferred
  • IFVG/FVG or OB entry
  • At least 4-5 strong confluences
  • Clear target identified
A
Good Setup
Solid trade with good confluences but missing 2 key elements or HTF alignment is not perfect. Still a tradeable setup but with more caution.
  • General bias known
  • PDA array entry present
  • 3-4 confluences aligned
  • Target is somewhat clear
B
Average Setup
Decent setup but missing several key confluences or entered without full top-down confirmation. These trades often work but are not ideal and should be reviewed carefully.
  • Some bias present
  • Entry on a PDA but not preferred
  • 2-3 confluences only
  • Target unclear or assumed
C
Weak Setup
Low quality trade. Entered without proper analysis or against the model. Often results in poor RR or stops being hit. These trades should be avoided entirely.
  • Bias unclear or ignored
  • Entry not on a clear PDA
  • 1-2 confluences only
  • No clear target
D
Poor Setup — Avoid
No business being in this trade. Emotional entry, revenge trade, FOMO, or complete disregard for the model. These trades damage the account and the mindset.
  • No HTF bias considered
  • Random entry with no PDA
  • Emotional or impulsive
  • Rule violations present

Key Terms & Concepts
PDA Array (Premium / Discount Array)
A zone on the chart where price is likely to react. Examples: FVG, IFVG, Order Block, Breaker Block, Mitigation Block. These are the core entry tools in ICT methodology.
IFVG — Inverse Fair Value Gap
A previously filled FVG that has been "inverted" and now acts as a support or resistance zone. Central to Dodgy's iFVG Ultimate+ model. Preferred entry for tightest stop placement.
FVG — Fair Value Gap
A 3-candle imbalance where the middle candle's body leaves a gap between the wicks of the first and third candle. Price tends to return to fill these gaps before continuing.
MSS — Market Structure Shift
A change in the direction of market structure. A bullish MSS occurs when price breaks above a prior swing high after a downtrend. A bearish MSS breaks a prior swing low. Used to confirm bias change.
SMT — Smart Money Tool / Divergence
When two correlated instruments (e.g. NQ and ES) fail to make the same high or low together. This divergence signals that one market is being manipulated and a reversal is likely.
Liquidity Sweep / BSL & SSL
BSL (Buy-Side Liquidity) sits above swing highs where stop losses cluster. SSL (Sell-Side Liquidity) sits below swing lows. Smart money sweeps these levels before reversing — this sweep is a preferred entry signal.
HTF PDA Delivery
Price delivering into a higher timeframe (Daily, 4H, 1H) Premium or Discount Array. This gives the trade a strong institutional reason for the reaction, greatly increasing confluence.
Kill Zone
Specific session windows where institutional order flow is highest. London Open (2–5 AM EST) and NY AM (9:30–11 AM EST) are the primary kill zones used in this journal.
Power of 3 (PO3)
ICT's model of Accumulation → Manipulation → Distribution within a session. Price first moves one way to grab liquidity (the Judas Swing), then reverses to deliver to the real target.
R:R — Risk to Reward
The ratio of potential loss (stop loss distance) to potential gain (take profit distance). A 1:3 R:R means risking $1 to make $3. Higher R:R setups are preferred, especially on A+ and above grades.
CISD — Change in State of Delivery
A concept from ICT where price shifts from delivering bearishly to delivering bullishly (or vice versa) at a key PDA level. On a 1m chart, a CISD looks like price tapping into a PDA, printing a strong rejection candle that closes above (or below for shorts) the previous range, signalling that delivery has flipped. It's often used as a precision entry confirmation after a liquidity sweep — price sweeps SSL, CISD prints bullish, then you enter the IFVG left behind by the CISD candle.
LRLR — Low Resistance Liquidity Run
A term used by Dodgy and other ICT traders to describe a clean, unobstructed path to a liquidity target. When there are no significant opposing PDAs, order blocks, or FVGs between current price and the target, price tends to move there quickly with little resistance. Identifying an LRLR above (for longs) or below (for shorts) before entering gives you a high confidence clear target — price has a clear runway to reach it. This is a strong confluence because it means your TP is likely to be hit without much chop.
